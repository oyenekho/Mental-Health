About Mental Health

45%

Only 45% of Generation Z individuals say that their mental health is good. This is 11% less than the next closest generation.

91%

91% of Generation Z individuals said they experienced a physical or emotional symptoms due to stress and mental illness.

1 in 3

Only 1 in 3 African Americans who need mental health care actually recieve it. When they do seek care, they often recieve a poorer quality of care.

1 in 25

Approximately 1 in 25 adults in the US experiences a serious mental illness in a given year that substantially interferes with major life activities.

1 in 5

Approximately 1 in 5 youth aged 13-18 experience a severe mental disorder at some point during their life.

1 in 9

Approximately 1 in 9 women experience at least one depressive episode in their lives. The most common mental health issue in women is depression and they are 2x as likely to experience depression in their lifetime as men.

Suicide Prevention

Unfortunately, suicide is the third leading cause of death for young adults. It is the 12th leading cause of death in the US overall. Suicide is a serious problem and prevention is very important.

Recognizing Suicidal Behavior

  • Severe sadness or moodiness. Long-lasting sadness, mood swings, and unexpected rage is one indicator.
  • Withdrawal. Choosing to be and avoiding all social situations are possible symptoms of depression, a leading cause of suicide.
  • Dangerous or self-harmful behavior. Dangerous behavior such as reckless driving and increased use of drugs or alcohol is an indicator that the person no longer values their life.
  • Making preparations. Often, a person considering suicide will start to put their personal matters in order. For example, they may start visiting all their friends and family, creating a will, and emptying their home.
  • Threatening or talking about suicide. From 50% to 75% of people considering suicid will give a warning sign. They may talk an increasing amount about death or talk of a world without them in it. Not every threat is easy to detect, but when detected should be taken seriously.

Suicide Prevention

  • Ask. Ask the individual if they are dealing with suicidal ideation. It is not an easy question, but it is proven that asking at-risk individuals if they are suicidal does not increase suicides or suicidal thoughts.
  • Keep them safe. Reducing a suicidal persons access to lethal items is key to suicide prevention. Asking if the at-risk individual has a plan and removing then removing the tools makes a difference.
  • Be there. It is importan to listen carefully to what the individual is thinking and feeling. Acknowledging and talking about suicide may reduce rather than increase suicidal thoughts.
  • Help them connect. Connect them with hotlines and other resources so that they can get the help needed. You can also help the connect with a mental health professional or another trusted individual. Doing so will ensure that they have someone to talk to.
  • Stay connected. Staying connected after a crisis shows that you care and it makes a difference. It is proven that the number of suicide deaths goes down when someone follows up with the at-risk individual.
